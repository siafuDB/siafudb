---
title: "Integrating with NTL"
description: "Swap GSPA for GSPN — route mutations as neural signals through the Nyuchi Transfer Layer."
---
Once you have a SiafuDB fragment syncing via GSPA, the upgrade path to GSPN is a transport swap. The mutation model, the schema, and the authority rules do not change — only how the signal gets from one fragment to the next.

## Prerequisites

- **NTL reachable on the network.** See [NTL — First Steps](https://ntl.nyuchi.com/research/06-first-steps).
- **IPv6** or dual-stack addressing. GSPN assumes neural addresses, which are IPv6-native.
- **Synapse topology** defined on the NTL side — where signals originate, where they terminate, what transforms attach to each synapse.

## Swap the transport

```rust
use siafudb::sync::Gspn;

let gspn = Gspn::new()
    .from(&pod)
    .to_synapse("synapse://platform-ingress")
    .build()?;

gspn.start().await?;
```

The emitter no longer worries about destinations. It hands the mutation to NTL as a signal; NTL decides which synapses fire and which transformations apply.

## Where transforms live now

Under GSPA, the emitter applies transforms before the wire. Under GSPN, transforms are attached to synapses:

| Synapse | Transform |
|---------|-----------|
| `synapse://platform-ingress` | `StripPII` |
| `synapse://analytics` | `Anonymise`, `Aggregate` |
| `synapse://owner-feedback` | `Anonymise` |

Moving transforms to the network means a single emitted signal can feed multiple destinations with different projections — no duplicate serialisation.

## Learned routing

Synapses retain weights. Frequently-travelled paths strengthen; cold paths decay. Over time, a deployment of SiafuDB fragments connected through NTL learns its own topology. See [NTL — Twelve Principles](https://ntl.nyuchi.com/research/05-twelve-principles).

## Fall-back

If NTL is unreachable, GSPN degrades to GSPA. The graph stays coherent; you lose adaptive routing until the neural layer comes back.
